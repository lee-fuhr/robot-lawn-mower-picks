Frequently asked questions

How long does a robot mower take to cut a large yard?

A robot mower doesn't cut the whole lawn in one pass the way a traditional mower does. It works on a schedule, mowing for two to four hours per session, returning to charge, and going out again, covering the yard incrementally each day. The cumulative result over a week is a consistently mowed lawn, but the mower isn't designed to do the whole job in one continuous run. For large yards, runtime per charge and charge time both matter: look for models with longer runtime per cycle and faster charging to maximize daily coverage.

Do large-yard robot mowers need a more complex boundary setup?

Yes, in general. A perimeter wire for a large yard requires more cable, takes longer to install, and can be tricky to plan around gardens, trees, and property edges. GPS-guided models reduce that complexity significantly for large yards since you trace boundaries on an app and can adjust them without physically moving wire. Many large-yard buyers find that the upfront cost of a GPS model is worth it primarily to avoid the installation burden rather than for any mowing quality difference.

What cutting width do I need for a large yard?

A wider cutting deck reduces the number of passes required to cover a given area, which matters most on large open yards with few obstacles. Models aimed at large yards typically offer cutting widths of 8–12 inches, compared to 6–8 inches on compact models. On a yard with lots of narrow passages, trees, or garden beds, a very wide cutting deck can become a liability, the mower has to make more careful maneuvers and may miss tight spots. Match the cutting width to the type of open space you have, not just the total square footage.
