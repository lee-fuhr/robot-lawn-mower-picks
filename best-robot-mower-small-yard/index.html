If you have a small yard, a robot lawn mower can free you from weekend mowing for good. Unlike larger properties where you worry about battery range, a small lot lets even entry-level models handle the job. Frequently asked questions

What counts as a small yard for a robot mower?

Most manufacturers treat anything under about 0.25 acres (roughly 10,000 sq ft) as the small-yard tier. At this size, a compact model with a narrower cutting width and shorter runtime handles the job well, and you don't need to pay for the larger coverage capacity of a premium unit. If your lawn is under 5,000 sq ft and relatively simple in shape, even the most entry-level robot mowers cover it comfortably on a daily schedule.

Is a perimeter wire worth installing for a small yard?

For a very small, simple yard, think a rectangle with no islands or obstacles, a perimeter wire is quick to install and the boundary won't need much adjustment. The wire cost is low and setup typically takes a couple of hours. For a small yard with irregular edges, garden beds to avoid, or obstacles like trees and planters, a wire-free GPS model can be easier to set up and adjust since you remap boundaries from an app rather than physically repositioning wire. Both approaches work; the deciding factor is how complex the edges are.

Can a robot mower handle a front yard and a back yard?

Most entry-level models are designed for one contiguous lawn area. Handling both a front and a back yard requires either running a passage between them (some models can navigate a passage as narrow as 24 inches) or buying a model explicitly designed for multi-zone operation with separate boundary definitions per zone. Check that the model you're considering supports multi-zone if your front and back yards don't connect.
